About this site

No Switzerland

Most IIoT software content is produced by the people selling IIoT software, or by the cloud providers and automation giants whose revenue depends on which architecture you choose. The operations leader trying to build a credible shortlist for a $300K platform deployment has no neutral ground to stand on. This site is an attempt to fix that.

Written for operators, engineers, and the people caught between them

IIoT software buying involves at least three distinct stakeholders who rarely agree. The COO or plant manager wants OEE improvements and a defensible capital expenditure. The controls engineer wants protocol compatibility, edge stability, and a guarantee that no software update will halt a production line. The CDO or digital transformation lead wants open architecture, scalable data pipelines, and a path to cloud analytics. Each of these buyers is right about what matters to them. The software market addresses all three with the same slide deck.

This site is written for all buyers. The goal is to give you better technical information, clearer evaluation frameworks, and vendor comparisons structured around the criteria that actually differentiate platforms, so your organization can move from pilot to production without the most common failure modes.

The picture, by the numbers

Pilot failure
~60%
IoT initiatives that stall at the proof-of-concept stage. Most fail not because the technology does not work, but because it cannot scale across heterogeneous legacy environments.
Annual downtime cost
$1.4T
Cost of unplanned downtime to the world's 500 largest manufacturers — equal to 11% of total revenues, up from $864 billion five years earlier.
Per-hour cost
$260K
Average cost per hour of unplanned downtime across manufacturing sectors. In automotive, the figure reaches $2.3 million per hour.

The pilot failure rate and the downtime cost are related. Organizations spend months and significant budget trialing platforms that ultimately cannot deliver visibility across mixed-protocol, multi-site environments, while the cost of the problem the software was supposed to solve keeps compounding.

What independent means on this site

This site does not favor cloud-native architectures over on-premise deployments, open-source approaches over commercial platforms, or hyperscaler ecosystems over independent vendors. Every architectural approach is evaluated on fit for the environment it targets.

Analysis is based on publicly available information, product documentation, practitioner input, and direct evaluation where possible. When the evidence for a claim is thin, this site says so.

The people making IIoT software decisions are trying to close a gap between the factory floor and the enterprise data layer that has existed for decades. They need better information than the market currently provides. That is the only reason this site exists.

Sources: Cisco, "Journey to IoT Value," 2017 (corroborated by Microsoft IoT Signals and HiveMQ 2024 surveys). Siemens, "True Cost of Downtime," 2024.
